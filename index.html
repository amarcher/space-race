The hero card

The Rescue Shuttle.

In Mille Bornes, the Emergency Vehicle (Right of Way) is the best card in the game — it shrugs off two different hazards and lets you keep driving without needing Roll after a Stop. Milo knows this, and he loves it.

In Space Race, that card becomes the Rescue Shuttle: white hull, red cross, yellow siren light, flame trail. It's the card every 5-year-old will fight over during the game — and the artwork kids will want framed on their wall after.

"If you have the Rescue Shuttle in your hand and someone plays a Black Hole on you, you slam it down. That's a coup-fourré."
— Milo, age 4, probably

Points structure preserved from Mille Bornes: +100 for each safety revealed, +200 for each coup-fourré (revealing it at the moment a hazard is played on you), plus the usual race-win bonuses.

The Counter-Thrust

The best moment in the whole game.

Mille Bornes' coup-fourré: an opponent plays a hazard on you, and you slam down the matching safety in the same moment — catching them. It's the bluffing layer that makes the game a game — do you reveal your safety early for the easy +100, or sit on it hoping someone triggers the +300? Milo at 4 already calculates this.

In Space Race we call it the Counter-Thrust — the literal English of coup-fourré, and now a word with real weight in this deck where thrusters show up everywhere. "Thrust" becomes the deck's signature motif, the way "tire" belongs to Mille Bornes.

Nano Banana · session script

The literal prompt sequence for the first batch.

The prompts from the manifest above are the what. This is the how — the order you run them in, which image becomes the style anchor, how to reference-feed it into later prompts, and when to accept vs regenerate. Getting the sequence right is how a 19-card deck ends up looking like one product.

1 · The two-session rule

Two completely separate Nano Banana chat sessions. Don't mix. The cosmic aesthetic and the angelic aesthetic are different enough that style cross-contamination ruins both — the safeties start looking gloomy, the hazards start looking precious. Fresh session = clean style.

Session 1

Cosmic · 15 cards

  • 5 distance · 5 hazards · 5 remedies
  • Anchor: Black Hole — lock the palette on the most cosmic subject
  • ~60–90 min · 45–75 generations (3–5 variants per card)
  • Everything in one chat — sequential, with anchor reference
Session 2

Angelic · 4 cards

  • All 4 safeties
  • Anchor: Rescue Shuttle — Milo's favorite; nail it first
  • ~30–45 min · 15–25 generations
  • Fresh chat — don't resume Session 1

Session 3 (~30 min): the three auxiliary pieces — tuck box background, card back tile, tuck box back panel — get their own fresh chat. Painterly cosmic only (no subjects, no hero portraits), so the aesthetic discipline is different. Reuse the Session 1 Black Hole anchor as a style reference, but start a clean thread. Prompts are in the Auxiliary Art section of the manifest below.

6 · Reference-image techniques

Three prompt patterns that keep style consistent across generations. Pick whichever hits hardest for the drift you're seeing.

Feed the anchor back

Once the anchor is locked, upload it to the chat and prefix every next prompt with an explicit reference directive:

"Generate a new image matching the style, palette, lighting, and mood of this reference image exactly. Subject: [X]."

Iterative refinement

Don't one-shot. Generate, look, then refine within the same message thread:

"Make the lighting more dramatic." / "Push the plasma highlights." / "Deepen the shadows to pure black." / "Remove the small bright spot in the top-right — distracting."

Palette-lock reminder

Every 3–4 prompts, re-anchor the palette with an explicit reminder — drift is sneaky:

"Keep the palette strictly to Void #06061A, Nebula #1A0933, plasma magenta #C879FF, ember red #FF5E5B. No other colors."

Negative prompts

Nano Banana respects "no X" directives if you say them clearly. Bake these into every prompt:

"No text, no logos, no UI, no watermarks, no lens flares, no distorted geometry, no hands or faces if not part of subject."

7 · Accept or regenerate

When a variant comes back, run it against both gates. If any pass criterion fails, or any regenerate criterion hits, don't force it — burn one more generation. Budget allows.

Accept if all 5

  • Palette matches locked tokens (within 5%)
  • Lighting matches the session mood (cosmic vs angelic)
  • Subject reads clearly at a glance
  • Composition works cropped to circular art-well
  • No text, logos, or AI artifacts visible

Regenerate if any

  • Palette drifted (reds too orange, violets too pink)
  • Safety looks dim / cosmic looks washed
  • Distorted or duplicated geometry
  • Subject sits too close to edge (will clip at crop)
  • Text, numbers, or UI elements snuck in
  • Two cards look identical — one has to differentiate

8 · Post-generation prep

What happens between "variant picked" and "card ready to export." Five steps per card, ~3–5 minutes each.

  • 01Download highest-resolution export from Nano Banana (usually 1024×1024 or 2048×2048)
  • 02Upscale to 1500×1500 if needed, using Topaz or similar — the art well needs detail at 300dpi print
  • 03Crop to square, centered on the subject, leaving ~10% breathing room around
  • 04Drop into Figma via the [art] instance-swap on Card/Master
  • 05Run Image Palette plugin on all 19 placed cards — flag any that drifted outside the locked palette

Total budget: ~90 generations across both sessions, 2–3 hours of focused prompting. Breaks help — Nano Banana style drift is partly your drift too. Pick the anchors when fresh; handle outliers when tired. The whole deck ends up looking coherent because one person, in two focused sittings, watched it stay coherent.

Aesthetic direction · revised

Less picture book. More mythic rare.

Full pivot from children's-book cartoon. The new direction: painterly, photorealistic, deep cosmic base across every non-safety card — purple-black-space with dramatic gold typography — and safeties as the only light cards in the deck. When a safety hits the table, it physically stands out. Think Marvel Snap mythic rares, vintage Chesley Bonestell, and Denis Villeneuve's chiaroscuro.

Distance
Luminous · lifting

A notch brighter than hazards and remedies. Cosmic still, but with more ambient star-glow and warm halation around the subject. 25 LY is a warm pinpoint; 200 LY a radiant galaxy. Think "approaching the safeties but never matching them" — the rising energy of distance makes progression feel visually earned.

Hazard
Evil · cosmic-horror

Not just moody — actively sinister. Alien (1979) over Star Wars. Blood-red emergency glow, oppressive dark, the subject itself looks cursed. Tractor Beam should feel predatory; Black Hole should feel hungry. The universe wants you dead, not just inconvenienced.

Remedy
Panacea · divine-tech

Technical but reverent — cure-all energy, not industrial. Warm teal with a faint halo, the object itself subtly glowing as if already partially sacred. Holy-tech hybrid: engineering that arrives as relief, a laying-on-of-hands feel. Not quite safeties — but a step closer to them than hazards.

Safety
Angelic · radiant

Photorealistic hero portraits. Blown-out white-gold light behind the subject. Halo rays. The only light cards in the deck — MTG mythic-rare energy. When revealed, the table gets visibly brighter.

The light gradient: hazards darkest → remedies a step up (warm halo hints) → distance a step further (bright cosmic luminosity) → safeties peak (angelic white-gold). Four clearly distinct moods on a shared axis, so fanning a hand reads like a dynamic-range ladder.

Bulk-replace titles/subtitles across all 19 cards in one sweep. Essential once the Nano Banana prompts settle and the text needs to hit every instance.

4 · The Milo playtest

Play one full game with him before you approve.

This is the real QA step. Milo is the only person who's played 200+ games of the source material; he'll catch what a print-only review can't.

Watch for four signals:

  • Which cards he reaches for first — art-quality signal. The angelic safeties should magnetize his attention.
  • Any "hard to see" reactions — legibility issue. Usually subtitle or value corner size.
  • Any "that's wrong" reactions — mechanic/theme mismatch. He knows the source game better than you.
  • The first Counter-Thrust moment — does it land as satisfying as a Mille Bornes coup-fourré? If yes, you've preserved the soul of the game. If no, the safety cards need more visual weight.

After the playtest + QA. Three buckets.

Clean
0 issues across all 30 checks.

Small fixes

Re-proof

Rules insert · generic

Rules for the game itself.

Neutral voice. No "Hi, I'm Milo" — this insert is part of the core deck and lives on the game shelf long after the party. Clear, instructional, third-person. Milo's voice belongs on the personalization card (see below), which slips in alongside this one and can be removed without harming the game.

1000 Light-Years

A galactic card game

The game

You are a pilot. The first player to fly 1000 light-years wins the round.

Setup

Shuffle the deck. Deal 6 cards to each player. Place the rest face-down as the draw pile. Choose who goes first.

Your turn

  1. Draw one card.
  2. Play one card or discard one.
  3. Pass to the next pilot.

To move

Play Ignition to start. Once ignited, play distance cards — 25, 50, 75, 100, or 200 light-years. Maximum two 200-cards per player, per game.

Hazards & remedies

An opponent may play a hazard on you. You cannot advance until you play the matching remedy.

Asteroid Strike→ Repair Drone
Empty Tank→ Fuel Cell
Busted Thruster→ New Thruster
Tractor Beam→ Beam Cutter
Black Hole→ Ignition

Safeties

Four safety cards in the deck. Reveal one to become permanently immune to its matching hazard for the rest of the round. +100 points each.

  • Ace Pilot — immune to Asteroid Strike
  • Antimatter Fuel Cell — immune to Empty Tank
  • Diamond Thruster — immune to Busted Thruster
  • Rescue Shuttle — immune to Tractor Beam and Black Hole

The Counter-Thrust

If an opponent plays a hazard on you and you hold the matching safety, reveal it immediately. That move is a Counter-Thrust. +300 points. The largest scoring play in the game.

Scoring

When one pilot reaches 1000 light-years, the round ends. Tally:

  • 1 point per light-year traveled
  • +100 per safety revealed
  • +300 per Counter-Thrust
  • +400 to the pilot who reached 1000 first
  • +300 if a pilot revealed all 4 safeties

Race to the stars. ✦

Note: no QR code on this insert. The rules card stays generic; the QR lives on a separate personalization card (the "Milo Reads the Rules" card) so the core artifact stays timeless.

5 · QR code · static vs dynamic

Static QR

URL is baked into the QR pattern permanently. Generated once (free via goqr.me). Works offline, no subscription, can't break.

Downside: if YouTube takes the video down, or you want to change the destination later, every printed tuck box is dead.

brittle but free

Dynamic QR

QR points at a redirect URL you own (Bitly free tier or a custom domain). You control the final destination forever — if YouTube dies in 2030, you change where the redirect goes, and the printed QR still works.

One-time ~5 min setup, same free cost. No one notices the difference when scanning.

recommended

Recommended setup: YouTube unlisted → short Bitly redirect (bit.ly/1000ly) → static QR generated from the Bitly URL. Not on the tuck box — the QR lives on the dedicated Milo Reads the Rules card (see the Personalization Layer section) so the core deck stays generic. Print at ~1.5″ square on the Milo card · high contrast (navy background, white QR) · test-scan with a phone before approving the proof.

Contingency: keep a backup copy of the video on iCloud and on an external drive. If a friend's kid scans it five years from now and it's broken, you'll want the re-upload to be a 5-minute job.
